This is what Evansgray is all about

Evansgray strives to put textiles into the world that are crafted with a care and quality worthy of becoming an heirloom piece. The combination of natural fiber and plant-based dyes build a foundation within each textile, like an honest blank slate for the years of memories to come.

A letter to you

Growing up in Memphis, TN, I learned the importance of the past at a young age. My mom taught me about the badass, oddball women of my family tree; my dad taught me to dissect, analyze, and experiment with anything that piqued my interest. I remember being a tiny little kid, curled up in their bed studying the lines of my great-grandmother’s quilts and listening to stories about her 92 years of life. There were so many moments sewn into this intricate work of art and so many markings from its years of use.

A textile’s ability to retain emotion is powerful.

The same quilt came with me to college, where I continued to study it as I learned about the history and creation of textiles. My interest piqued at the mention of natural dye. By the time I finished one simple onion skin dye bath, I was hooked. It is a process that requires acknowledgement and appreciation of the past, a meditative process requiring one’s full attention, and the foresight to develop upon millennia of historical record.

Every few years, I sit down with that quilt and mend any new tears. I want it to live forever.

Good and bad, heartwarming to heart wrenching, I have learned the intrinsic value of referencing tradition when looking to the future. Slow art reminds us to be present in the moment, learn from the past, and create a sustainable future. Evansgray is my contribution to the world. I want to create wholesome, functional items that are worth mending and passing along to our future great-grand children.

With love,

Emily Harrison

Why natural dye?

I fell in love with natural dye as an art student in 2012. With that first onion skin dye bath, I knew I’d found my passion. Natural dyeing combines elements of the cooking, chemistry, experimentation, and slow art practices that fuel my drive to create.

I adore the hands-on, slow-moving practice of natural dye, where time becomes an ingredient in color development. Natural colors have a certain depth and subtlety that cannot be achieved by other means. Every natural color tells a story. I dedicate my studio practice to the experimental nature of plant based dyes and how they can be incorporated into our everyday lives. Over time, I hope to continue learning the intricacies of natural dye while sharing natural colors with the world.

An extrodinarily brief history of natural dyes

Early natural dyers - dating back to, at least, the advent of agriculture over 12,000 years ago - likely discovered their colors through cooking. Madder, weld, indigo, and walnut were recorded as known dyestuffs by the end of the first century BCE, all four of these dyes can be found as components of many Evansgray colors. One of the most remarkable aspects of natural dye is the further development of color in conjunction with a textile’s age and use. Synthetic dyes became readily available as a cheaper, faster alternative to traditional dye techniques in the late 1800s. Natural dye processes faded into relative obscurity. As artists shift their interests toward a return to slower, more thoughtful processes, natural dye is seeing a resurgence in popularity.
